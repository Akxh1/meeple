The complete data pipeline transforms a small set of real student interaction records into a statistically grounded synthetic dataset suitable for training a mastery-level classification model. The pipeline comprises five major stages:

2. Real Data Collection

2.1. The Data Collection Instrument

Real-world behavioural data was captured using a purpose-built Mock Exam Application — a web-based assessment tool designed to simulate a high-stakes exam environment. Unlike standard quizzes that record only final answers, this instrument logged granular behavioural interactions in real time:

  • Temporal Dynamics: Time per question, hesitation latencies, total duration
  • Navigation Patterns: Tab switching frequency, answer revision rates, question review markings
  • Cognitive States: Self-reported confidence ratings (1–5 Likert scale) and hint usage patterns
  • Performance Metrics: Score percentage, hard question accuracy, performance trend (1st vs 2nd half)

2.2. Data Collection Batches: From 55 to 77 Real Records

Data was collected in two batches from participants (primarily university students) who voluntarily took the mock exam:

Batch Source File Records Period Notes
Batch 1 student_research_data.csv 51 Dec 2025 – Jan 2026 Initial collection round (~55 unique participants after accounting for retakes)
Batch 2 student_research_data_2.csv 57 Dec 2025 – Feb 2026 Expanded dataset: includes Batch 1 + new participants (Feb 2026 additions), bringing unique total to 77
How 55 became 77: The initial real dataset contained approximately 55 unique student records (Batch 1). A second round of data collection in February 2026 added approximately 22 new participants, bringing the total unique real data pool to 77 records. Batch 2 (student_research_data_2.csv) is the combined superset used as the statistical basis for synthetic generation.

3. Synthetic Data Generation: 77 Real → 2,000 Synthetic

3.1. Why Synthetic Data?

  • Insufficient Real Data: 77 records are too few to train a robust multi-class ML classifier (typical minimum: 500–2,000).
  • Privacy Protection: Synthetic data contains no identifiable individual records.
  • Class Balance Control: Enables producing balanced representation across mastery levels.

3.2. Technique: Cholesky Decomposition

The Cholesky Decomposition method was chosen because it preserves the exact inter-feature correlation structure observed in the real data. This means the synthetic students exhibit the same realistic relationships between features as real students (e.g., students who use more hints typically take longer per question and have lower confidence).

Step-by-Step Process:

1
Extract Statistics from Real Data: Calculate the mean (μ), standard deviation (σ), and full Pearson correlation matrix (R) from all 11 features across all real records.
↓
2
Regularise Correlation Matrix: Add a small regularisation term (0.001 × I) for numerical stability and positive-definiteness: R' = R + 0.001 × I
↓
3
Cholesky Decomposition: Decompose R' into a lower-triangular matrix L such that R' = L × LT. This matrix L encodes all inter-feature correlations.
↓
4
Generate Uncorrelated Samples: Draw 2,000 × 11 random samples from a standard normal distribution N(0,1). These are independent, uncorrelated values.
↓
5
Induce Correlations: Multiply uncorrelated samples by LT to produce correlated samples: Xcorrelated = Xuncorrelated × LT
↓
6
Scale to Original Distribution: Transform from N(0,1) to real data’s distribution: Xfinal = Xcorrelated × σ + μ
↓
7
Apply Realistic Constraints: Clip each feature to its valid domain range (e.g., score_percentage: 0–100, avg_confidence: 1–5) and round to 2 decimal places.
R' = R + 0.001I   →   L = cholesky(R')   →   Xsynth = (Z × LT) × σ + μ
Why Cholesky over SMOTE or GANs?
• SMOTE interpolates between existing samples — requires a class label beforehand and can create outliers at class boundaries.
• GANs require much larger datasets to train the generator/discriminator and are prone to mode collapse.
• Cholesky is deterministic, reproducible (seed=42), preserves exact correlation structure, and works well with small N. It is the standard approach in educational data mining for small-sample augmentation.

3.3. Validation of Synthetic Fidelity

After generation, the synthetic dataset is validated by comparing:

  • Mean/Std Alignment: Synthetic means and standard deviations closely match real data.
  • Correlation Matrix Preservation: The synthetic 11×11 correlation matrix mirrors the real one (core guarantee of Cholesky).
  • LMS Distribution Comparison: Real vs synthetic mean LMS is compared for consistency.

4. Target Variable: Learning Mastery Score (LMS)

4.1. Three-Stage Formula Derivation

The LMS formula — converting 11 raw features into a single mastery score — was developed through a rigorous three-stage process:

Stage 1: Literature-Based Formula (Initial)

LMSinitial = 0.50(S) + 0.15(Hd) + 10(Ccal) + 10(Ks) + 10(Af) − 15(Hu)1.5

Each weight is justified by specific research papers (Pardos & Baker 2014, Chi et al. 2018, Aleven et al. 2016, Tobias & Everson 2002, Baker et al. 2004).

Stage 2: Data-Driven Correlation Analysis (N=56 Real Students)

Since no ground-truth LMS exists (the formula itself defines mastery), supervised regression would be circular. Instead, four unsupervised methods were applied to derive empirical feature weights:

score_percentage 27.4% 25.6% 31.7% 17.7% 25.6% Positive ↑
avg_confidence 16.5% 23.2% 12.9% 13.4% 16.5% Positive ↑
tab_switches_rate 20.5% 5.3% 17.0% 17.8% 15.2% Negative ↓
hint_usage_percentage 6.2% 13.4% 5.0% 19.6% 11.0% Negative ↓
answer_changes_rate 1.9% 3.9% 1.5% 13.1% 5.1% Negative ↓

Bootstrap resampling (n=1,000) confirmed weight stability despite moderate sample size.

Stage 3: Refined Hybrid Formula (Production)

LMShybrid = 0.30(S) + 0.25(Hd × 100) + 15(Ccal) + 15(Af) − 10(Hu) − 5(Ac)
Component Literature Data-Driven Hybrid Rationale
Score (S) 0.50 25.6% 0.30 Data: S and Hd near-equal; reduced dominance
Hard Accuracy (Hd) 0.15 26.6% 0.25 #1 differentiator across all 4 methods; elevated
Calibration (Ccal) ×10 16.5% ×15 Metacognitive weight increased per data
Attention (Af) ×10 15.2% ×15 r=0.50 with hard accuracy — stronger than expected
Hint Usage (Hu) -15×Hu1.5 11.0% -10×Hu Lower impact than literature; simplified to linear
Answer Changes (Ac) ×10 5.1% -5×Ac 75% near-zero variance; lowest discriminator
Validation: All six features maintain the same positive/negative direction across both literature and data-driven analyses, confirming empirical soundness of the theoretical framework.

6. Phase 1: Bootstrapping Architecture (Current Research Phase)

To overcome the “cold start” problem inherent in educational AI (lacking thousands of fully annotated student records on day one), the current architecture relies on synthetic generation and a formulaic target label. This phase proves that the underlying Predict → Explain → Act framework is technically viable.

77 Real Students (Mock Exam App)  →  11 Behavioural Features  →  Cholesky Decomposition (preserves correlations)  →  2,000 Synthetic Records  →  Hybrid LMS Formula (acting as the oracle)  →  XGBoost Classifier (90.75% accuracy)

7. Phase 2: Reusable Production Pipeline (Future Roadmap)

In a true production environment, the synthetic data generation and the formulaic target variable are completely removed. The pipeline becomes a self-sustaining loop powered by real-time data and continuous, organic learning.

While the formulaic target (Phase 1) is rigid and requires complete data, the Machine Learning model acts as a robust Surrogate Model. It learns complex, non-linear interactions between variables (e.g., high scores combined with high latencies) which a fixed formula misses, and it can resiliently generate predictions even if real-time data streams drop certain telemetry features.

7.2. Production Data Flow

P
Predict (Real-Time Inference): A student finishes a mock exam. The Laravel backend aggregates their raw telemetry into an 11-feature vector and sends it to the Flask ML API. The XGBoost model immediately evaluates the vector and returns a Mastery Level prediction (e.g., At-Risk).
↓
E
Explain (SHAP Context): The same 11-feature vector is passed through the SHAP TreeExplainer. It generates a local explainability payload detailing exactly why the prediction was made (e.g., “Hint usage was unusually high (80%) and latency was above average”).
↓
A
Act (LLM Intervention): The Mastery Level and the SHAP context are sent to the Large Language Model. Using the specific weaknesses identified by SHAP, the LLM generates a highly personalised, adaptive hint targeted precisely at the student’s behavioural deficits.
↓
↻
Continuous Learning Loop: The new, authentic student record is saved to the production database. As hundreds of students use the system, the dataset grows organically. Periodically, the formulaic target will be replaced by actual end-of-year exam results (ground truth), and the XGBoost model will be retrained, shedding all reliance on Phase 1 methodology.

9. Methodological Considerations & Limitations

  • Small Real Sample Size (N=77): While Cholesky faithfully preserves correlations, the estimated correlation matrix from 77 records has inherent sampling uncertainty. Bootstrap validation (n=1,000) confirms weight stability.
  • Gaussian Assumption: Cholesky decomposition assumes multivariate normality. Some features (e.g., hint_usage_percentage) may have skewed distributions. Clipping to valid ranges mitigates unrealistic tail values.
  • No Ground Truth for LMS (Phase 1): The mastery score is a constructed metric, not externally validated. The unsupervised data-driven analysis (Stage 2) provides empirical grounding but cannot replace external criterion validation (Phase 2).
  • Synthetic ≠ Real: The synthetic data preserves first and second-order statistics (means, correlations) but may not capture higher-order patterns, multimodal distributions, or rare outlier behaviours present in real data.
  • Reproducibility: Random seed is fixed at 42, ensuring deterministic output. Re-running the script produces identical results.
